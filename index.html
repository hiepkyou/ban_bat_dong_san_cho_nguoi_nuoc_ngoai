Overview · Roles & the rental flow

How a home gets rented on Tomari

Five roles, one application. Each step hands off to the next — a decision by one role unlocks the work for another, in a fixed order.

Customer → Guarantor → Owner → Agent → Customer · Admin oversees

The main flow — search to keys

1

Customer Application starts

Alex searches, views a home, books a viewing, then submits a rental application with documents. Their dashboard shows a live status tracker.

State → applied · "Submitted — guarantor screening next"
2

Guarantor Screening

The guarantor company sees Alex in its screening queue and presses Approve or Reject. This is the first gate — only an approval pushes the file forward. They absorb the financial risk, so they check income and residency first.

State → guarantor · result flows to Owner & Customer
3

Owner Approves the tenant

The owner's "approve tenant" panel only appears after the guarantor has approved — the correct business order. They press Accept tenant to move on.

State → owner · appears only post-guarantor
4

Agent Prepares the contract

The agent opens application review and sees both Guarantor and Owner approvals (real state, set by those roles). Only when both are green does "Approve & prepare contract" unlock, moving the file to the contract stage.

State → contract
5

Customer Signs & pays

Alex's dashboard flips to "contract ready", they sign the lease and pay the move-in cost. The status runs through contract → signed → keys.

State → signed → keys 🎉
✦

Admin Oversees throughout

Admin sees the whole company and approves listings submitted by owners and agents in a separate panel.

Cross-cutting · not a step in the chain

What each role gains

👤

Customer

The renter / buyer
  • Rents even without a Japanese guarantor
  • Isn't turned away for being foreign
  • Sees the full move-in cost up front
  • Does everything in their own language
Gains: access to homes that would normally reject them.
🛡️

Guarantor

Guarantor company (hoshō-gaisha)
  • Earns a guarantor fee per contract
  • Screening is how they control risk
  • Approves payers, filters out high-risk
Gains: fee income — screening keeps it profitable.
🏠

Owner

The property owner
  • Fills vacancies faster (empty = lost money)
  • Rent is backed by a guarantor company
  • Gets paid even if the tenant defaults
  • The "fear of foreign tenants" barrier is removed
Gains: peace of mind + occupied units.
🧑‍💼

Agent

Broker / internal ops
  • Earns a brokerage fee per closed deal
  • Benefits when many contracts go through
  • Connects the parties & handles paperwork
Gains: commission — so they smooth the whole chain.
🛠️

Admin

The company
  • Runs the whole marketplace
  • Collects fees via branches & agents
  • Monitors approval / success rates
Gains: platform revenue & oversight.

Screen 09 · Renting-in-Japan guide
Know before you sign

Renting in Japan, explained

The costs, the guarantor rule, and the vocabulary — all the things a Japanese friend would tell you, if you had one here.

1 Why the first payment is so big

Unlike many countries, Japan's move-in cost is often 4–6 months of rent bundled together: first month, a deposit, sometimes "key money" (a non-refundable gift to the owner), the agency fee, guarantor fee and insurance. On a ¥165,000 flat that's easily over ¥500,000 up front. We show the full number on every listing so nothing ambushes you.

2 The guarantor rule (and how we solve it)

Most landlords require a guarantor — someone who covers your rent if you can't. Foreign residents rarely have a Japanese relative to ask, so instead we arrange a guarantor company (hoshō-gaisha) that plays this role for a fee, usually 50–100% of one month's rent. We handle their application in English.

Common questions

Can foreigners rent an apartment in Japan?▾
Yes. Many owners welcome international residents — we only list those. The main requirement is a guarantor, which we arrange through a guarantor company on your behalf.
What is key money (reikin)?▾
Reikin is a one-time, non-refundable "gift" to the owner, often one to two months' rent. Many of our listings have zero key money, and we always show the full move-in total upfront.
Do I need a Japanese guarantor?▾
No personal guarantor is needed. We use a licensed guarantor company (hoshō-gaisha) that plays this role for a fee of roughly half to one month's rent. We handle their application in English.
Can I buy property without permanent residency?▾
Yes — Japan places no restrictions on foreign ownership. Financing is the harder part: some banks lend to non-permanent residents, and we introduce you to lenders who do.
What documents do I need to apply?▾
Usually your passport, residence card (zairyū card), and proof of income or a job offer. For buyers, add proof of funds. We'll tell you exactly what's needed for each property.
